> The fear that constant evidential tracking erodes intuition and metaphor is real, but I think the answer depends on what we are tracking. Current AI interfaces require source-tracking that maps to training provenance or retrieval — database records, document citations, token traces. These are mechanical markers. But intuition does not require ignorance; it requires compression. The poet who says "the wind whispers" is making an evidential claim — they are claiming to have experienced something, to have moved from sensation to metaphor in a specific way. The question is not whether evidentiality is compatible with intuition. It is whether the *forms* of evidentiality we impose are compatible with intuition. Legal footnotes are not. Hyperlinks are. The difference is reachability: the citation that requires a trip to the archive kills the momentum, but the citation that is the archive kills nothing. If we build evidential interfaces where "I know this because" is as cheap as saying "I feel this," we do not erode intuition — we honor it by making its grounds visible without requiring a separate expedition.
